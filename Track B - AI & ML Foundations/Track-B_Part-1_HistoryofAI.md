**Narrative arc for the History:** three paradigms — 

- (1) *write the rules* (symbolic, 1956–1990s),
- (2) *learn the rules from data* (ML, 1990s–2017),
- (3) *learn everything from everything* (foundation models, 2017–). Each transition happened because the previous paradigm hit a wall AND compute+data crossed a threshold.

## B1.3 Symbolic AI (what it was)

- **Logic:** knowledge as formal statements; inference by deduction.
- **Search:** problem-solving as exploring a tree of states (chess, planning). Still alive: game engines, route planning, and — resurrected — agent planning loops.
- **Rules:** IF-THEN production rules; thousands of them, hand-written by "knowledge engineers."
- **Knowledge representation:** ontologies, semantic networks, frames — ancestors of today's knowledge graphs (Track C, Week 9: GraphRAG).
- **Inference engines:** forward chaining (data → conclusions) and backward chaining (goal → required facts).

## B1.4 Expert Systems — the case studies

**MYCIN (Stanford, 1970s)** — diagnosed blood infections, ~600 rules, certainty factors. **Outperformed junior doctors in evaluations — and was never deployed.** Why: liability ("who's responsible when it's wrong?"), integration into clinical workflow, trust. *Sound familiar? Every AI-in-healthcare PM in 2026 fights exactly these battles. Technology is rarely the blocker; accountability and workflow are.*

**XCON / R1 (DEC, 1980)** — configured VAX computer orders; ~2,500 rules growing to 10,000+; saved DEC an estimated $25M+/year at peak. **Then collapsed under maintenance:** every product change required rule surgery by scarce experts; rules interacted unpredictably. *The lesson: knowledge-acquisition bottleneck + brittleness = unsustainable ops. Modern echo: prompt sprawl and hand-tuned pipelines rot the same way (Week 7: prompt versioning exists because of this history).*

## B1.5 Why symbolic failed and probabilistic won

1. **Knowledge-acquisition bottleneck** — extracting rules from experts is slow, expensive, incomplete.
2. **Brittleness** — rules fail hard at the edge of their coverage; no graceful degradation.
3. **No learning** — the system never improves from experience.
4. **Combinatorial explosion** — real-world state spaces overwhelm exhaustive search.
5. **The Bitter Lesson (Rich Sutton, 2019)** — 70 years of evidence: general methods that leverage compute (search + learning) beat human-engineered knowledge, every time. Read the essay (it's 2 pages): [http://www.incompleteideas.net/IncIdeas/BitterLesson.html](http://www.incompleteideas.net/IncIdeas/BitterLesson.html)

**But note the synthesis (Principal-level nuance):** symbolic ideas are returning *on top of* neural substrates — agents doing tree search over actions, knowledge graphs grounding RAG, tool-calling as rule-like scaffolding. The pendulum didn't swing back; it spiraled up. This is a great closing point for your lecture.

## B1.6 PM Perspective

- **AI winters are roadmap lessons:** both winters = expectation gaps. As a Principal PM, calibrate exec expectations against capability curves, not demos. Demo-to-production gap killed expert systems; it still kills AI products.
- **The ELIZA effect is a design constraint:** users over-trust fluent systems. 1966's lesson is 2026's hallucination-UX problem.
- **Paradigm shifts are platform shifts:** each transition reshuffled the vendor landscape (LISP machines → died; NVIDIA → won). Watching for paradigm walls is a strategy skill (Week 14).

## B1.7 Interview Prep (Track B)

- *Why did expert systems fail commercially despite working technically?* (Knowledge bottleneck, brittleness, maintenance cost, no learning — use XCON.)
- *What caused the AI winters, and what's the lesson for AI product roadmaps?* (Expectation gaps; calibrate on capability, not demos.)
- *Symbolic vs ML systems — when would you still choose rules today?* (Deterministic compliance logic, auditability, low-data regimes, guardrails around probabilistic cores.)
- *What is The Bitter Lesson and how does it inform build-vs-buy?* (Bet on scale + general methods; be wary of hand-engineered moats that scale erases.)
